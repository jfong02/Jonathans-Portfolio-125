About Me

I'm a third year Cognitive Science student at UC San Diego pursuing a B.S. in Design and Human-Computer Interactions. Some courses I've taken involve the study of neuroscience, human cognition and computer programming as well as applications in team-based design centered courses. When I'm not coding or creating wireframes and storyboards, you'll find me playing violin and guitar and trying out new places to eat around town.

Why Design?

To me, design is not just how to make the best looking interactive app, or the product that has the most impressive features. It’s really about finding out specific problems and needs that users and clients have, and addressing those problems in a variety of different ways. I’ve learned that having a need-based mindset to find solutions in various aspects of the workplace and at home is essential for gaining insight into ways to increase efficiency and understanding in how data and information is better processed, understood, and utilized.

My Story

During my undergraduate education studying the wide, interdisciplinary scope of cognitive science coupled with my specialization of design and interaction, I experienced how the design process is centered on a need-based problem-finding mindset in various aspects of our daily lives. I have been exposed to many areas of the design process, from needfinding and ideating, to creating personas and storyboards, to different degrees of fidelity in prototyping, to implementation and coding.

These skills have been applied in several projects, including a mobile app to connect volunteers and the elderly to assist the elderly in their grocery needs, which you can check out my design process details here or click the top logo, as well as a mobile site to improve college students’ cooking experience by utilizing leftover groceries in the kitchen and finding matching recipes in an intuitive and easy to use site. Critique

"Pencils Before Pixels"

As designers, we constantly strive to make connections between different ideas, statistics, or representations, and making the intangible tangible. Yet in order to make these connections, there are countless different ways to think about these connections. One of the ways that Mark Basinger from Carnegie Mellon University considers to be the most powerful is with the mighty pencil. In Basinger’s article "Pencils Before Pixels", he discusses why the pencil and drawing/writing are so crucially important in the design process. Basinger explains how it achieves 3 goals in a coherent, effective manner:

  1. To externalize and convey the process of thinking—to transform intangible ideas to tangible information for others

  2. To reveal ideas/relationships, not results

  3. To engage discussion around the subject/problem as an inclusive activity.

    4. - (Basinger 29)

Drawing or sketching ideas out allows designers to think critically about a specific idea visually, spatially, and temporally. By crafting a visual story, it “[shifts] verbal and written communication toward a compelling sketch or visual narrative” and enables designers to communicate more clearly about specific relationships and ideas and convey their thought process effectively (Basinger, 35). I consider drawing ideas out to be fundamentally more powerful in the design world than spoken or written communication as well. A written narrative rarely conveys the story or connection as efficiently as a simple sketch of a particular experience could. A scenario with a variety of crucially important perspectives and settings can be described all within a few frames of a storyboard, whereas a written statement must spend significant time and words to sufficiently describe the same scenario to the point where a designer can understand the connection in the scenario.

Another point Basinger brings up is that these drawings allow designers to reflect on a certain idea without any limitations of finality or substantiality. He discusses how “Too often are drawings viewed as final artifacts, where in the mind of the author they should be protected;” rather, they should be “seen as negotiable ideas that invite others into the conversation to ask questions and share their ideas as well” (Basinger 31). The great thing that I regard about drawing is that drawing engages others with a simple but effective method that doesn’t hold any particular value other than as a representation of the designer’s thought process. Ideas are always evolving and changing, and in order to develop an idea to the fullest potential, designers must be willing to give up a particular direction if another direction is discovered to be more viable. This change in thought can come from other designers reflecting on a particular idea presented in a drawing, or even from another drawing by the same designer.

Without this fundamental thought process of sketching, it would be significantly more difficult to ideate and collaborate on an idea than if sketches of ideas, situations, and people were brought into the discussion. This is why I consider Basinger’s argument to be worthy of mentioning. By putting down an idea onto paper, particularly in a drawing, it describes the situation and the many relationships in the setting of the idea and opens up the floor for discussion regarding that idea. This can lead to other drawings or diagrams, and produces a more cohesive and complete design process as designers collaborate to discover and ideate to address problem spaces.
